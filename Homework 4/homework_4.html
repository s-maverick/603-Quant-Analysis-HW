house_price_model <- function(x1, x2, actual_price) {
  predicted_price <- (-15000 + 25000 * x1 + 15000 * x2)
  residual <- actual_price - predicted_price
  return(c(predicted_price, residual))
}

Multicollinearity is a concern in regression analysis when two or more explanatory variables are highly correlated, which can distort the estimated coefficients and inflate standard errors. In this context, both year (years in current rank) and ysdeg (years since highest degree) are likely to be highly correlated, since faculty typically begin their academic careers soon after earning their degree. Including both variables in the same model could therefore lead to multicollinearity, making it difficult to isolate the individual effect of each predictor. To avoid this issue, we excluded ysdeg from the model and retained year, as it is more directly tied to the salary progression within academic ranks. Regarding the hypothesis that the new Dean offers more generous salaries, the coefficient for new_dean is 2163.46 with a p-value of 0.0496, which is just below the typical 0.05 threshold for statistical significance. This suggests moderate evidence in support of the hypothesis: faculty hired under the new Dean earn, on average, $2,163 more than those hired before, controlling for degree, rank, sex, and years in current rank. Therefore, we find support for the claim that the new Dean has been offering higher salaries to newly hired faculty.

The model with the interaction term better represents the relationship between size, new, and the outcome price. In the model without interaction, the effect of size on price is assumed to be the same for both new and not new homes—only the intercept changes based on whether the house is new. However, the interaction model allows the effect of size to differ depending on the home’s condition, which is more realistic. From previous results, we saw that the price difference between new and not new homes grows as size increases, which suggests that the impact of size is not uniform across both categories. This pattern indicates a meaningful interaction: new homes gain more value per additional square foot than not new homes. Therefore, the interaction model is preferred because it captures this changing relationship, leading to more accurate predictions and a better fit to the data when such differences exist.
